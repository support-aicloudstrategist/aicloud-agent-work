Pattern Studies

Four representative scenarios we are built to solve — drawn from seven-plus years of hands-on engineering across Indian SMB and enterprise cloud estates, combined with industry-standard benchmarks from the FinOps Foundation, Google SRE, DORA, and the major cloud providers. Each one reflects the waste, risk, and recoverable spend we consistently find in accounts that have not been formally reviewed. Real client engagements — anonymised or NDA-covered — will replace these as our first retainers conclude.

Pattern 1 — D2C e-commerce on AWS, ₹30–40L/month cloud spend

Typical setup: 80–150-person company on AWS ap-south-1 for 3+ years. CFO notices a 30–40% year-on-year cloud-cost increase that outpaces revenue growth. No formal FinOps function. Tagging inconsistent. Reserved Instance coverage below 20%.

What we expect to find: 30–45% of EC2 compute on idle or oversized instances after a festival-season scale-up that was never reversed. 30–60 TB in S3 Standard holding data untouched for 12+ months. 20–50 orphaned EBS volumes after EC2 terminations. No resource tagging, so cost-per-team or cost-per-product is unknowable.

What we would do (4-week sprint): Right-size 50–70% of the EC2 fleet to correctly-sized instances based on p95 CPU and memory over 30 days. S3 lifecycle policies moving cold data to Glacier Instant Retrieval. Cleanup automation for orphaned EBS and stale snapshots. A Reserved Instance plus Savings Plan blend sized for 70–80% baseline coverage. Infracost wired into the GitHub pull-request pipeline. OpenCost deployed for EKS workload visibility. Tagging enforced via Service Control Policies.

Expected outcome: a verified 20–30% reduction on the monthly bill (typically ₹6–12L/month at this spend band), confirmed across two full billing cycles. An ongoing cost baseline owned by the engineering team. A cost-per-order metric in the CFO's monthly review.

Pattern 2 — B2B SaaS on multi-region GCP, ₹15–25L/month cloud spend

Typical setup: 30–60-person Series A company running a multi-tenant product on GKE across two or more regions. The second region usually exists because of an early customer requirement, while the traffic reality has since shifted. Cost-per-tenant is not measured; no committed-use discounts in place despite a stable baseline.

What we expect to find: GKE node pools provisioned for peak QPS that occurs only a few times per quarter, leaving 60–75% idle headroom 24×7. 2–5 TB of monthly cross-region egress because the analytics warehouse sits in the wrong region relative to the query origin. 10–25 persistent disks unattached for 90+ days. Cloud SQL instance oversized by 3–5× after a historical load test that was never rolled back.

What we would do (4–6 week sprint): Cluster autoscaler tuning with correct PodDisruptionBudgets and a custom HPA on actual QPS. Replicate the analytics warehouse into the primary region with scheduled sync. Disk reclamation cron with 7-day quarantine before deletion. A 1-year committed-use discount on the stable India baseline. Cloud SQL right-sized after 30 days of observation. A cost-per-tenant dashboard built on BigQuery billing export.

Expected outcome: a 25–30% cloud-bill reduction (typically ₹4–7L/month), sustainable with zero customer-facing incidents during transition. Cost-per-tenant visible to the engineering team. New feature pull requests include cost impact estimates from Infracost.

Pattern 3 — AI-first startup on AWS with a GPU fleet, ₹18–30L/month AI spend

Typical setup: 20–40-person seed-to-Series-A generative AI product. Inference on SageMaker real-time endpoints. Fine-tuning on p4d or p5 instances on On-Demand. Feature store on DynamoDB or Redis. GPU spend has grown 3–4× in two quarters, and leadership has no board-ready answer for the AI infrastructure line item.

What we expect to find: Real-time inference endpoints over-provisioned for 90th-percentile traffic that occurs a few hours a day — typically a full GPU class larger than needed. Fine-tuning jobs running on On-Demand when spot capacity is 70–85% available at a 60–70% discount. No inference caching on endpoints with highly repetitive prompts (summarisation, categorisation, guardrail checks). DynamoDB provisioned capacity running at 10–15% utilisation. Model artifacts in S3 Standard when most are cold after a new version ships.

What we would do (3–4 week sprint): Right-size real-time endpoints, one GPU class down with autoscaling. Migrate 70–85% of fine-tuning workloads to spot with checkpointing and tested recovery. Add a semantic cache (Redis or OpenAI-compatible) for repetitive inference. Switch DynamoDB to on-demand. S3 lifecycle rules moving older model artifacts to Glacier Instant Retrieval. Build a cost-per-1,000-inferences dashboard per endpoint, tagged by product feature.

Expected outcome: a 35–45% reduction in AI infrastructure spend (typically ₹7–12L/month at this band). GPU idle time down from ~45% to under 10%. Cost-per-1K-inferences visible against product feature margins — the number every founder building an AI product needs before the next pricing decision.

Pattern 4 — Fintech or SaaS platform, DevOps maturity bottleneck

Typical setup: 40–70-person Series B company. Deployments take 40–90 minutes, require a senior engineer on-call, and are informally banned on Fridays. New engineers take 4–6 weeks to reach their first production deploy. "Staging" has drifted from production for over a year. MTTR on production incidents exceeds two hours.

What we expect to find: A monolithic CI pipeline running 25–40 minutes with no caching or parallelism. Deployments executed via shell scripts on a bastion host — no IaC for the deployment mechanism itself. Terraform coverage between 40% and 60% of production resources, with the rest a mix of CloudFormation remnants, console edits, and one person's private playbook. Secrets baked into AMIs or environment variables. Policy-as-code absent: `:latest` image tags reaching production.

What we would do (4-week sprint): Rebuild CI pipelines in GitHub Actions or GitLab with matrix parallelism and aggressive caching. Deploy ArgoCD, migrate 4–6 priority services to GitOps with progressive rollout. Build a Terraform module library and raise coverage to 90%+. Integrate AWS Secrets Manager. Install OPA Gatekeeper policies on the cluster. Integrate Infracost into pull requests. Ship a lightweight internal developer CLI (devctl) for service scaffolding and preview environments. Two enablement sessions with the full engineering team, including a live rollback rehearsal.

Expected outcome (measured 60 days post-delivery): Lead time for changes cut from 7–10 days to under a day. Deployment frequency 4–6× higher. Change failure rate roughly halved. MTTR dropped by 60–75%. Team moves from DORA "Low" to "High" tier on three of four metrics. Friday deploys become routine.

How a typical engagement runs

Seven steps from first contact to signed case study. Fixed price, fixed scope, no surprises.

1 · Discovery call (30 min, free)

30-minute structured call. You tell us about your stack, monthly spend, what triggered the conversation. We tell you what we would scope, what we would not scope, and whether we are the right fit. You leave with a clear go / no-go recommendation — even if the answer is no.

2 · Proposal (same business day)

If there is a fit, we send a fixed-price proposal within the same business day. Scope, deliverables, timeline, payment milestones, data-handling clauses. Valid for 14 days. No upsell, no scope-creep traps.

3 · Kickoff (week 1, day 1)

90-minute kickoff call with your engineering lead and finance contact. Read-only IAM access provisioned. Pre-read checklist confirmed. Shared Slack or WhatsApp channel opened for async communication. NDA already signed before this step.

4 · Discovery and diagnosis (week 1)

Six-month cost baseline pulled, commitment coverage analysed, waste inventory built, tagging review completed, architecture cost patterns flagged. Daily async updates. One mid-week check-in if you want it.

5 · Quantification and plan (week 2)

Every finding quantified in ₹/month. Every action classified by implementation effort and risk. Ranked backlog with sequencing. 90-day implementation plan with named engineering and finance owners. Senior-architect review on every finding before delivery.

6 · Final readout (week 2, day 10)

90-minute decision meeting — not a slide show. Your engineering lead, finance contact, and any executive stakeholder join. Recording provided. Slides handed over. Open-source tooling (Infracost, OpenCost, FOCUS billing export) handed over to your git repository.

7 · Optional follow-on (month 2+)

Implementation Sprint if you want us executing the backlog, Managed FinOps Retainer if you want ongoing oversight, or a clean hand-off if your team is ready to execute alone. Gain-share available on the retainer — aligned incentives, not hourly billing.

Questions from actual discovery calls

The questions engineering leaders and CFOs have asked most often in the first 15 minutes. Answered directly, with the context we would give on the call.

“We already bought CloudHealth / Flexera / Apptio. Why you?”

Those tools surface data. Someone still has to act on the data. Most teams we engage have a dashboard installed but no one with the 30 hours per month to interpret it, build the RI purchase models, and drive execution. That is the gap our engagement closes. We integrate with your existing tool rather than displace it — many engagements begin with a QuickStart that specifically acts on findings the client's existing tool has been flagging for months.

“Can you guarantee 20-30% savings?”

No, and anyone who does is misleading you. What we can guarantee: a ranked backlog with verified ₹ figures, a fixed-price engagement fee, and our honest read on whether your account is in the typical 20-30% recoverable range or not. In every audit we have run, the range has held. If yours were to be the first exception, we would tell you on day 4.

“How do you handle the conflict of interest with gain-share?”

Three structural safeguards. First, a floor — the gain-share only kicks in above verified monthly savings (typically ₹1L/month). Second, a cap — total gain-share capped at 2x the annual retainer fee, so we cannot 10x-bill on a one-time finding. Third, an exit — either party can terminate with 60 days' notice after month 6; post-termination gain-share only applies to savings already realised, not future ones.

“What if you find things we knew about but never fixed?”

That is the majority of what we find. The value is not in the discovery — it is in the prioritisation, the ₹-quantification, and the implementation plan with named owners. Teams usually know they have RI coverage below target. They do not have a monthly governance cadence that makes fixing it routine. That is the thing the engagement installs.

“How do we verify your senior-architect oversight is real?”

The oversight layer signs off on every major finding in the engagement deliverable. If you want, we can arrange a 15-minute call with the reviewer before you sign — same model big firms use for reference calls on partners. Founder-led means Anushka's hands on the work; enterprise-reviewed means someone with four times her experience validates every recommendation before it reaches you.

“Are you going to try and upsell us into a retainer?”

Only if it makes sense. On the final readout call we tell you what we saw, what we recommend, and what the three paths forward are (clean hand-off, Implementation Sprint, Managed Retainer). We are as comfortable saying “your team can execute this alone, save the retainer fee” as we are recommending ongoing work. The gain-share structure on the retainer means we only win when your bill actually goes down — which filters out engagements where a retainer does not create real value.
